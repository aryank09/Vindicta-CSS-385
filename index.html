About the Game

The Fall of Olympus

When the ancient titan Chronos breaks free from the depths of Tartarus, his wrath consumes Mount Olympus. The mighty gods Zeus, Poseidon, and Hades are cast into the darkest corners of their own realms, imprisoned by chains of time itself.

As a brave hero, you must venture through three treacherous domains to free each god and claim their Divine Blessings - the only power capable of standing against Chronos.

Gameplay & Mechanics

Vindicta is a retro-inspired action-adventure game that challenges players to battle through the fallen realm of Olympus. Each freed god grants you unique abilities that stack and evolve, creating a progressive power system that transforms both exploration and combat as you advance toward the final confrontation with Chronos.

Inspiration & Influences

Drawing inspiration from classic 16-bit masterpieces like Castlevania and Metroid, combined with the epic storytelling of Greek mythology. The authentic pixel art style pays homage to the golden age of gaming while bringing ancient legends to life in vivid detail.

Divine Blessing System

  • Free Zeus to unlock devastating lightning attacks and aerial mobility
  • Rescue Poseidon to command water currents and storm barriers
  • Liberate Hades to manipulate shadows and traverse the underworld
  • Combine divine powers for devastating combo attacks against Chronos

Playtesting Reports

Early Development Playtest (Week 3)

Development Phase: Core mechanics implementation

Test Method: In-person observation

Participants: 5 students (3 CS majors, 2 non-CS), ages 19–22

Key Feedback:

  • Missing character sprites: Playtesters noted that there were no visual sprites for the player or enemy, making it hard to engage with the combat system.
  • Spam-Attack imbalance: Because “Attack” was always available without drawback, players just spammed Attack—undermining the intended rock-paper-scissors dynamic (Attack beats Grab, Grab beats Defend, Defend beats Attack).

Changes Made:

  • Rebalanced move costs: Increased the cost (or cooldown) of repeated “Attack” inputs so that spamming Attack no longer guaranteed success. This ensures players consider Grab or Defend strategically.

Mid-Development Playtest (Week 6)

Development Phase: Level design and UI implementation

Test Method: Remote testing via screen share (Discord)

Participants: 7 participants (mixed gaming backgrounds: casual, indie-dev, competitive)

Key Feedback:

  • Still missing polished sprites: Players commented that the placeholder art made it difficult to feel immersed—“we need proper character visuals.”
  • Combat felt too chance-based: Several testers said they were rolling dice rather than making strategic choices; the game lacked a “meaningful trade-off” and felt random.

Changes Made:

  • Added Rest mechanic: Introduced a “Rest” option that recovers a small amount of health but costs you a turn. This gives players a deliberate choice between offense and defense rather than relying on pure luck.
  • Added sprite/background files: Added sprites to improve visual clarity and player engagement alongside level backgrounds for respective bosses.

Final Playtest (Week 9)

Development Phase: Polish and final adjustments

Test Method: Structured gameplay

Participants: 12 players (ages 18–22; varying skill levels)

Detailed Feedback:

Combat Transparency
  • Players and the professor both requested that the Boss’s available move set be displayed on-screen alongside our active buffs. This would allow for more strategic planning rather than guessing what the enemy might do next.
  • Eight out of twelve testers specifically said that knowing the enemy’s next possible actions made them feel more in control, rather than feeling “caught off-guard.”
Buff Clarity
  • Most testers agreed that displaying active buffs (e.g., “Soul Shield: Heal +1 on Defend,” “Tidal Pull: Grab +1 dmg”) under the player’s health bar helped them decide when to use Defend or Grab.
  • Two players recommended showing a tooltip or small icon animation whenever a buff activates, but overall they felt the current text-only method was sufficient.

Planned Changes:

  • Start Screen/Button: Implement a start screen with a “Play” button that leads into the first level, improving onboarding and flow.
  • Cutscenes: Introduce brief opening and mid-battle cutscenes to set context before fights and highlight key story moments.
  • Ending Scene: Create a closing sequence and credits screen once the cyclops is defeated, giving a satisfying conclusion to the game.

Future Recommendations:

  • Introduce a brief tutorial overlay on first launch showing how rock-paper-scissors interactions work (e.g., “Attack beats Grab, Grab beats Defend, Defend beats Attack, Rest recovers HP”).
  • Consider adding a “combo gauge” that fills when players successfully predict and counter the cyclops’s move, encouraging deeper strategic thinking.

Team Reflection

What is one thing your team did really well?

Our team communicated clearly and pitched in wherever needed. Although each of us had a main role (backend logic for Aryan and Veer; frontend art and animations for Rajbir and Mario), anyone would jump in to help without hesitation if someone got stuck. That flexibility kept us moving forward without delays.

Evidence: Our commit history shows several instances where team members edited each other’s files to fix bugs or add features.

If you had 2 more weeks, what would your top 3 priorities be?

  1. Enhance Visual Indications for Buffs

    We would add clearer on‐screen cues whenever a buff activates—for example, having the player sprite glow gold when “Soul Shield” is active, a brief pop‐up text like “Grab+2 Damage” when Tidal Pull triggers, and a small health‐bar pulse when a healing buff kicks in. This ensures players immediately understand the impact of each buff without having to check numbers manually.

  2. Add More “Action” to Animations

    We would make character sprites move slightly toward their target when they Attack or Grab, such as a small lunge forward—and add a brief camera shake on heavy hits. At the same time, we’d smooth out frame transitions so each animation (idle, attack, hurt) looks more fluid. These tweaks would make combat feel more dynamic and engaging.

  3. Fix Death Logic and Return to Start

    We would correct the game logic so that when the player’s health reaches zero, the scene immediately fades out and resets to the starting screen instead of freezing or crashing. This ensures a seamless experience, once the player dies, they’re returned to the beginning of the level with a brief “Try Again” prompt.
